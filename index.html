Climate Vulnerability in India




India is the seventh most vulnerable country with regards to climate extremes (Germanwatch2020, cited in Mohanty & Wadhawan, 2021). The image on the left is from the 2023 floods in Assam, a state in the North-Eastern region of India that is ranked have the highest climate vulnerability.












About 75% of districts in India are hotspots for extreme events. The map on the right shows extreme event hotpots at the district level for the country. The nine states with a coastline – Gujarat, Maharashtra, Goa, Karnataka, Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, Odisha, and West Bengal – are specifically highlighted, and showcase that almost all the coastal districts (95%) are hotspots for at least one extreme event (Mohanty & Wadhawan, 2021).

The Climate Vulnerability Index, or CVI, is a micro-level vulnerability assessment that maps the climate vulnerability for the states in India (Mohanty & Wadhawan, 2021). It takes exposure (likelihood of hazards to occur), sensitivity (hazard’s impact on people and livelihoods), and adaptive capacity (ability to cope from the hazard’s impacts), the three key elements of vulnerability into consideration.


Climate Vulnerability Index of States in India

The index falls in a range from 0 – 1 where higher values (closer to 1) indicate higher vulnerability and lower values (closer to 0) indicate lower vulnerability. The legend in the map above is at 0.1 intervals from 0 to 0.6+ because the highest index value for a state is 0.616. The five most vulnerable states are Assam, Andhra Pradesh, Maharashtra, Karnataka, and Bihar. There are a few states that have an index value of 0 denoting that there is no climate vulnerability, suggesting an uncertainty in the data’s reliability.

Climate Vulnerability Index of Assam

The state of Assam in the North-Eastern zone of India has the highest vulnerability index score. The map above shows the CVI for the districts in Assam. Dhemaji and Nagaon have the highest scores of 1. The legend is at 0.2 intervals from 0 to 0.8+ to evenly contain all the values from the lowest index of 0 to the highest index of 1. There are some districts for which no data was available which are shown in the grey color on the map.

More on the Climate Vulnerability Index (CVI)

The CVI is based on IPCC’s SREX framework. It examines the intersections between vulnerability, exposure, and hazards, and how they collectively contribute to the disaster risk, as well as the influences and implications of natural climate variations, climate change as a result of anthropogenic forces, and development trends on ecosystems and human communities (Field et al., 2012).
